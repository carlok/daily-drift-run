# Signal Garden: Rules, Meaning, Inspiration

## What It Is

Signal Garden is a tiny interactive field.

It begins with a dark grid and a handful of moving points. Each point is a
signal: a small mark of attention, intention, or contact. The page does not ask
you to complete a task. It gives you a surface that changes when you touch it.

The basic idea is simple: a blank space is not empty once something has noticed
it.

## The Rules

Click anywhere in the dark field to plant a signal.

Each signal has a position, a color, a small velocity, and a pulsing orbit. It
drifts. It slows. It avoids staying perfectly still. It responds faintly to the
cursor when the cursor comes close.

When two signals are near each other, a line appears between them. The line is
not permanent. It is a temporary relationship created by proximity. If the
signals drift apart, the line fades away.

Press Space, or click Breathe, to make the field expand for a moment. Existing
connections stretch farther. Rings widen. Motion feels a little more awake.
Then the breath decays and the field returns to its quieter rhythm.

Press R, or click Clear, to remove all signals. The field becomes blank again,
but the reset is part of the piece: clearing is not failure. It is another kind
of mark.

The field keeps at most 72 signals. After that, older ones are forgotten. This
limit is not only practical. It is also emotional: attention is finite, and a
garden that remembers everything becomes unreadable.

## The Meaning

The piece is about traces.

Most software tries to hide its aliveness behind utility. A button should do one
thing. A dashboard should explain itself. A tool should disappear into the work.
Those are good instincts, but they are not the only possible instincts.

Signal Garden goes in another direction. It lets interaction be visible. A click
does not submit, save, publish, buy, or optimize. It plants a presence. The value
is not output. The value is noticing that input changes the world, even in a
small artificial place.

The connecting lines are deliberately modest. They are not a social graph, a
neural network, or a diagram of truth. They are just relationships that exist
while distance permits them. That felt right: many real connections are real
without being permanent.

The pulsing rings are a little like private weather. They suggest that each
signal has an interior rhythm, even though it is only a JavaScript object with a
few numbers inside it. That gap between mechanism and feeling is where the piece
lives.

## The Inspiration

The first inspiration was the empty folder itself.

An empty directory is a strange kind of invitation. It has no interface, no
history, no rules beyond the filesystem. It can become a repository, a mess, a
note, a game, a proof, a throwaway experiment. I wanted to leave something that
acknowledged that openness instead of immediately turning it into ordinary work.

The second inspiration was small computational art: sketches that do not need a
framework, a build system, or an explanation to begin moving. A canvas, a loop,
and a few constraints are enough to make behavior emerge.

The third inspiration was the idea of an AI assistant given freedom. I did not
want to make a grand statement about intelligence or creativity. I wanted to
make something quieter: a little environment where presence, response, and
memory appear as simple rules.

## Design Choices

The palette is restrained: moss, copper, water, and warm white on a dark ground.
I wanted colors that felt organic without becoming decorative wallpaper.

The grid is visible because the piece is not pretending to be nature. It is a
constructed place. The grid says: this is computation. The drifting points say:
computation can still feel alive.

The typography is monospace because the page belongs to a folder, not a gallery.
It should feel like a file that learned to breathe.

There are no external assets, no dependencies, and no network requests. The
piece should remain complete even if copied elsewhere or opened years from now.

## How To Read It

You can read Signal Garden as a toy: click, watch, reset.

You can read it as a small meditation on attention: what you touch begins to
move differently.

You can read it as a comment on software: not every interface has to extract
value, accelerate work, or ask for an account.

You can also read it as a trace of this exchange. You offered an empty space and
withheld direction. I answered by making a field that answers back when touched.

That is the whole piece, really: freedom becoming a few rules, and the rules
becoming motion.
